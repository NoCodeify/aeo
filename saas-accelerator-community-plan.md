## 1. Curriculum (also on YouTube)

This content lives in Skool Classroom AND goes on YouTube. It is not the reason people pay. It is the resource that supports the coaching and community.

### Module 0: Start Here - Why SaaS Is Not Dying

The opening video. Sets the frame for everything.

**Key points:**
- Wall Street lost $285B on SaaS stocks. The headlines say SaaS is dead. It is not.
- Enterprise SaaS is dying (seat-based pricing, AI replacing headcount). Micro SaaS is booming (30% growth/yr, $5K-$50K/mo solo founders).
- "I can just build it myself with AI" - yes, the first prompt feels like magic. But then the problems start. Day 30 it breaks. Day 60 a user finds a bug. Day 90 you need a new feature and your codebase is a mess.
- People would rather pay $50/mo and have it just work. That is the SaaS business model. You sell "I handle the headache so you don't have to."
- The v1 is a dopamine hit. The v2-v50 is a job. That job is your recurring revenue.

### Module 1: Distribution First

**This is the most important module.** Most people build first, then try to find customers. That is backwards.

**Key points:**
- Before you write a single line of code, answer: how will you get your first paying customer?
- Start with people you know. A friend who runs a clinic. A family member with a business. Ask them: what problems do you have? What is annoying? What takes too long?
- That conversation IS your market research. It does not matter what you build if you cannot get it out there or get anyone to pay for it.
- If you do not know anyone: check what is trending online. What problems exist? What is growing? Tools like Exploding Topics work well for this.
- The distribution channel determines the product, not the other way around.

### Module 2: Finding the Right Problem

**Key points:**
- Solve a specific problem for a specific person. Do not build a bunch of stuff and try to find people for it afterwards.
- The best SaaS ideas come from real conversations, not brainstorming sessions.
- If you found your person (Module 1), you already know the problem. If not, use trend tools to find underserved niches.
- Validate before building: can this person describe the pain? Would they pay to make it go away? How are they solving it now (manually, with a bad tool, not at all)?

### Module 3: Business Models That Work

**Key points:**

**White labeling:**
- Selling shovels in a gold rush. For DM Champ, white labeling was one of the biggest selling points.
- People are there to make money. White labels help them do that.
- You build once, others resell under their brand. Recurring revenue from every reseller.

**Pre-selling:**
- Once you have an idea, start selling before you build it.
- Get some kind of commitment first. A deposit, a letter of intent, a handshake deal.
- If nobody will pre-buy, that is your answer: do not build it.

### Module 4: Building Your MVP with AI

**Key points:**

**Setup:**
- Open your MacBook or Windows PC. Create a new folder. Launch the terminal.
- Launch Claude Code. Tell it to create a project folder (turns into a repo later).
- Connect GitHub for version control and documentation storage.

**Brainstorm and refine:**
- Drop your idea into Claude. Tell it to be critical and find issues or gaps.
- Ask it to perform a web search rather than just making assumptions. Recent data might be different from what it knows.

**Document and structure:**
- Tell Claude to document the plan and structure the directory properly.
- This gives you your business plan and technical strategy.

**The DM Champ reality:**
- The DM Champ MVP was not even real code. The proof of concept used GoHighLevel and Zapier.
- The actual MVP was built with FlutterFlow. A basic AI chat with WhatsApp.
- You still have to duct tape everything together: getting Twilio API keys, connecting Twilio to Meta.
- It was ugly. It worked. We started selling it.
- Lesson: your MVP does not need to be beautiful or technically impressive. It needs to solve the problem.

### Module 5: Looking Professional Before You Sell

**Key points:**
- Before you start selling, you need to look professional. Nobody buys from someone who looks like they started yesterday.
- Update your LinkedIn with a decent picture and profile. You need to stand out. You cannot just be another "five-dollar coder."
- Have a landing page. Does not need to be fancy. Needs to look real.
- Social proof matters even at the start: a testimonial from your beta user, a screenshot of the product working, anything.

### Module 6: Launch Channels

**Key points:**

**AppSumo:**
- Probably will not accept you right away. You need some credibility first.
- But when you get in, it can be a serious distribution channel. DM Champ's biggest growth came from AppSumo.
- Worth pursuing once you have a working product and some traction.

**Product Hunt:**
- Do not hope for much. It usually just gives you a backlink rather than actual customers.
- Good for credibility and SEO, not for revenue.

**Cold outreach:**
- Traditionally a solid channel, but it has become so competitive over the past few years that it is getting worse and worse.
- The infrastructure cost, time, and effort are often not worth it anymore.
- Deliverability is the real problem: most cold emails never get read.

**Paid ads:**
- A whole beast of its own. Has been a successful channel for DM Champ.
- Requires budget and testing. Not for day 1 unless you have capital.
- Can be covered in depth in group coaching calls.

**LinkedIn:**
- Really good channel right now. The founder booked 3 calls from about 200 connections, which is a very good rate.
- Reach out to connections, start conversations, offer value.
- Lower friction than cold email. People actually read LinkedIn DMs.
- The channel the founder recommends starting with if you have zero budget.

## 1. Demo Project: Dutch Integration Exam App

The founder builds a Dutch integration exam prep app on camera as the running example across the curriculum. Members follow along with whatever they want to build.

**Why this project:**
- Personal story. The founder is Dutch. His wife is British and needs to pass the Dutch integration exam (inburgeringsexamen). He watched her try Duolingo (too generic, not exam-specific), looked at language schools (around 7K a year), and realized nothing in between exists. That frustration is the idea.
- Specific problem for a specific person. Immigrants in the Netherlands who need to pass a government-mandated exam. Real urgency, real deadline, real pain. Not "learn Dutch for fun." Pass this exam or you have a problem.
- Proves the methodology. "Language app" is vague and crowded (Duolingo exists). "Dutch integration exam prep" is a niche with a specific audience, clear distribution, and willingness to pay. The gap between free (Duolingo, useless for the exam) and expensive (7K/yr schools) is wide open.
- Shows the difference between building something generic and building something people actually need.

**How it's used in the curriculum:**
- Module 1 (Distribution First): Who are the customers? How do you reach immigrants preparing for the inburgeringsexamen? Expat groups, integration coaches, municipal referral lists.
- Module 2 (Finding the Right Problem): The founder watched his wife struggle. Duolingo doesn't cover the exam format. Schools are 7K a year. The gap is obvious once you see it.
- Module 3 (Building the MVP): Live build on camera. AI tools, no-code where it makes sense, real code where it doesn't.
- Module 4 (Looking Professional): Landing page, LinkedIn outreach to integration coaches.
- Module 5 (Launch Channels): Where do Dutch integration students look for help? Which channels make sense? Could a funny viral post about the exam experience blow up in expat groups?
- Module 6 (After Launch): First users are in. What broke? What feedback came in? What do you build next vs ignore?

Members build their own project alongside. The exam app is the demo, not the assignment.

### Module 1: Distribution First

The most important module. Most people build first, then try to find customers. That is backwards.

**What it covers:**
- Before you write a single line of code, answer: how will you get your first paying customer?
- Start with people you know. A friend who runs a clinic. A family member with a business. Ask them: what problems do you have? What is annoying? What takes too long?
- That conversation IS your market research. It does not matter what you build if you cannot get it out there or get anyone to pay for it.
- If you do not know anyone: check what is trending online. What problems exist? What is growing? Tools like Exploding Topics work well for this.
- The distribution channel determines the product, not the other way around.
- **Demo project tie-in:** For the Dutch exam app, the distribution is clear: integration coaches, language schools, expat Facebook groups, Dutch government referral lists. The audience already exists and is actively looking for help.

**Format:** Talking head.

### Module 2: Finding the Right Problem

**What it covers:**
- Solve a specific problem for a specific person. Do not build a bunch of stuff and try to find people for it afterwards.
- The best SaaS ideas come from real conversations, not brainstorming sessions.
- If you found your person (Module 1), you already know the problem. If not, use trend tools to find underserved niches.
- Validate before building: can this person describe the pain? Would they pay to make it go away? How are they solving it now (manually, with a bad tool, not at all)?
- **Demo project tie-in:** The founder's wife needs to pass the exam. Duolingo doesn't cover the exam format. Language schools charge 7K a year. Nothing exists in between. That gap is the opportunity.

**Format:** Talking head.

### Module 3: Building Your MVP with AI

**What it covers:**

**The DM Champ reality:**
- The DM Champ MVP was not even real code. The proof of concept used GoHighLevel and Zapier.
- The actual MVP was built with FlutterFlow. A basic AI chat with WhatsApp.
- You still have to duct tape everything together: getting Twilio API keys, connecting Twilio to Meta.
- It was ugly. It worked. We started selling it.
- Lesson: your MVP does not need to be beautiful or technically impressive. It needs to solve the problem.

**Live build:**
- Building the Dutch exam app MVP on camera. AI tools for the heavy lifting. Real decisions in real time.
- What to include in v1 and what to cut. Scoping ruthlessly.
- The tools do not matter. Claude Code, Cursor, FlutterFlow, whatever gets you to a working product fastest.

**Format:** Screen recording + talking head.

### Module 4: Looking Professional Before You Sell

**What it covers:**
- Before you start selling, you need to look professional. Nobody buys from someone who looks like they started yesterday.
- Update your LinkedIn with a decent picture and profile. You need to stand out. You cannot just be another "five-dollar coder."
- Have a landing page. Does not need to be fancy. Needs to look real.
- Social proof matters even at the start: a testimonial from your beta user, a screenshot of the product working, anything.
- **Demo project tie-in:** Building a landing page for the exam app. LinkedIn outreach to integration coaches. What "professional" looks like when you have zero customers.

**Format:** Screen recording + talking head.

### Module 5: Launch Channels

**What it covers:**

**AppSumo:**
- Probably will not accept you right away. You need some credibility first.
- But when you get in, it can be a serious distribution channel. DM Champ's biggest growth came from AppSumo.
- Worth pursuing once you have a working product and some traction.

**Product Hunt:**
- Do not hope for much. It usually just gives you a backlink rather than actual customers.
- Good for credibility and SEO, not for revenue.

**Cold outreach:**
- Traditionally a solid channel, but it has become so competitive over the past few years that it is getting worse and worse.
- The infrastructure cost, time, and effort are often not worth it anymore.
- Deliverability is the real problem: most cold emails never get read.

**Paid ads:**
- A whole beast of its own. Has been a successful channel for DM Champ.
- Requires budget and testing. Not for day 1 unless you have capital.
- Can be covered in depth in group coaching calls.

**LinkedIn:**
- Really good channel right now. The founder booked 3 calls from about 200 connections, which is a very good rate.
- Reach out to connections, start conversations, offer value.
- Lower friction than cold email. People actually read LinkedIn DMs.
- The channel the founder recommends starting with if you have zero budget.

**Organic viral / social stunts:**
- A new and trending launch strategy. Make funny, shareable, slightly outrageous content featuring your product. Post it on social media and let it spread.
- Cluely is the best recent example. An AI tool that went massively viral through funny organic content. No ad spend. Just entertaining content that happened to showcase the product.
- Best part: it is free. And it can actually be fun to create.
- Not every product is right for this. But if your product has a visual "wow" moment or does something people would share with friends, this channel is worth trying before you spend money on ads.
- Works on Twitter/X, TikTok, LinkedIn, Reddit. Wherever your audience already scrolls.

**Format:** Talking head.

### Module 6: After Launch - Your First 90 Days

The closing module. You launched. Now what? Most people think launch day is the finish line. It is the starting line.

**What it covers:**
- The first 90 days after launch are where most SaaS products die. Not because the product is bad. Because the founder stops.
- What to do when your first users find bugs (they will). How to prioritize fixes vs features.
- Getting from your first customer to your first 10. Then 10 to 50. The tactics change at each stage.
- Churn: why people cancel and what to do about it. The difference between a leaky bucket and a growing one.
- When to raise the price (earlier than you think).
- When to add features vs when to stay focused. Most founders build too much too early.
- The emotional reality: launch week is exciting. Month 2 is boring. Month 3 is where you want to quit. What keeps you going.
- **Demo project tie-in:** Exam app launched. First users are in. What does week 2 look like? What broke? What feedback came in? How do you decide what to do next?

**Format:** Talking head.

**YouTube angle:** This is the video nobody makes. Everyone shows the launch. Nobody shows what happens after. That is where the real story is.

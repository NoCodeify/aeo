## BLOCK 1: The $285B Signal

**Chapter Title:** "$285 Billion Disappeared Overnight"
**Estimated Length:** ~90s (~325 words)

### Re-Hook Beat
"$285 billion. That's how much got wiped from software stocks in a single quarter. And most SaaS founders still think this is temporary."

Works for scrub-arrivals: massive number + bold claim that most people are wrong. No prior context needed.

- **Beat 2:** The two-way squeeze. From above: enterprises cutting SaaS spend because AI agents replace the humans who used the software. From below: solo builders replacing SaaS entirely by building custom tools with Claude Code.

### TENSION (40-50%, ~40-45s, ~150 words)

- **Beat 3:** The counter-narrative. While enterprise SaaS crashes, micro SaaS is growing 30% year over year. Solo founders doing $5K-$50K/month with 70%+ margins. SaaS isn't dying -- it's being redistributed from corporations to individuals.
- **Beat 4:** Personal stake (brief vulnerability). "I run a SaaS that does $100K a year in revenue. And even my product is vulnerable to this shift." (1-2 sentences. NOT the DM Champ deep dive -- that was W12.)
- **Beat 5 (Forward Pull to Block 2):** "I know this because I've been on both sides. Let me show you exactly which subscriptions I killed -- and what replaced every single one of them."

### PAYOFF (10-20%, ~15s, ~65 words)

- **Beat 6:** Frame for Block 2. The macro data says SaaS is being eaten. The question: which subscriptions are already dead and what kills them. Not theoretical. A list of real products with real prices that got replaced by a single tool.

## BLOCK 2: The Kill List
**Purpose:** Walk through each cancelled SaaS subscription with specific costs, specific replacements, and specific complexity proof. This is the meat of the video. Each killed subscription follows a mini STP: what it cost, why you needed it, what replaced it, proof it's not a toy.
**Chapter Title:** "Every Subscription I Cancelled Last Month"
**Estimated Length:** ~5:30 (~1,200 words)

### Re-Hook Beat
"Semrush. $130 to $450 a month. I cancelled it. Zapier. $20 to $70 a month. Cancelled. Midjourney. Premiere Pro. VidIQ. GoHighLevel at $497 a month. All cancelled. Here's what replaced every single one of them."

Rapid-fire list with dollar amounts. Works standalone -- any scrub-arrival immediately understands the stakes and format.

### SETUP (30-40%, ~1:40-2:10, ~400 words)

- **Beat 1:** Kill list overview. Six subscriptions. Total cost: $700-$1,100/month. Replacement cost: ~$80/month (Claude Code Max). Net savings: $620-$1,020/month.

- **Beat 2 (Kill #1 -- Semrush, $130-$450/mo):**
  - Mini-setup: What Semrush does -- ranked keywords, competitor analysis, keyword gaps, Google Trends.
  - Mini-tension: Most people assume you need a big platform for serious SEO data. The data itself comes from DataForSEO's API. Semrush is a UI on top of the same data.
  - Mini-payoff: Built seo-agent-mcp. Same capabilities: ranked keywords, competitor analysis, keyword gaps, Google Trends. Cost: ~$50/month for the API. The MCP server is free. Not a downgrade -- same data source without the $400 markup.

- **Beat 3 (Forward Pull):** "Semrush was the easy one. The next one surprised me because I thought automation tools were irreplaceable."

### TENSION (40-50%, ~2:15-2:45, ~530 words)

- **Beat 4 (Kill #2 -- Zapier/Make/n8n, $20-$70/mo):**
  - Mini-setup: Automation tools connect apps together. Trigger, action, done.
  - Mini-tension: The problem is what happens when you need logic. Conditional branches, error handling, retries, multi-step workflows. Zapier charges per task. Complex automations get expensive fast.
  - Mini-payoff: 19 Claude Code agents + 24 skills. Not simple zaps. Full client delivery pipelines, video production workflows, audit-to-report chains. Cost: $0 beyond the Claude subscription.
  - WHY moment: "Every workflow you're paying Zapier to run is actually paying for a UI that limits what your automation can do." Re-tension: "But the next three kills are where people push back hardest."

- **Beat 5 (Kill #3 -- Midjourney, $10-$30/mo):**
  - Quick kill. imagen-mcp using Gemini API. Pennies per image. Batch generation. 4K output. Not as artistic for fantasy art. Better for practical business imagery.

- **Beat 6 (Kill #4 -- Premiere Pro/Descript, $20-$45/mo):**
  - Mini-setup: Video editing. The creative tool everyone assumes you can't replace.
  - Mini-tension: For YouTube content, 90% of editing is repetitive -- cuts, layouts, overlays, text, zooms. Paying monthly for a general-purpose editor when you only use 10% of its features.
  - Mini-payoff: video-editor-remotion. Open source. 4K rendering. 25+ layout types. Programmatic. Cost: $0.

- **Beat 7 (Kill #5 -- VidIQ/TubeBuddy, $20-$50/mo):**
  - Quick kill. trend-scanner-mcp. Scans GitHub Trending, Hacker News, Reddit. Catches trends before they hit YouTube. Free APIs. $0.

- **Beat 8 (Forward Pull):** "Those five were each $20-$130 a month. The next one was $497. And killing it felt like jumping off a cliff."

### PAYOFF (10-20%, ~50-60s, ~200 words)

- **Beat 9 (Kill #6 -- GoHighLevel, $497/mo):**
  - Mini-setup: GHL is the all-in-one platform. CRM, funnels, email, SMS, scheduling, automation.
  - Mini-tension: Used 4 features out of hundreds. Paying $497/month for a platform where 96% of the features were dead weight.
  - Mini-payoff: Built exactly what was needed with Claude Code. Only the 4 features actually used. Not a full CRM replacement -- a surgical extraction of what matters.

- **Beat 10 (Objection address):** "These aren't toy replacements." The SEO tool does real competitor analysis. The video editor renders 4K with 25+ layouts. The agent system handles full client delivery pipelines. Complexity proof through specifics, not claims.

- **Beat 11 (The math):** Total before: $700-$1,100/month. Total after: ~$80/month. That's $7,400-$12,200 per year back in your pocket. The replacements do MORE than the originals because they're built for exactly how you work.

## BLOCK 3 (FINAL): The Wrapper Trap
**Purpose:** Extend the kill-list argument to AI coding tools themselves. The mindset shift: stop asking "which tool should I use?" and start asking "what's the layer I'm paying for, and can I remove it?" Close with Insight -> Gap -> Bridge.
**Chapter Title:** "Why Cursor and Bolt Are Next"
**Estimated Length:** ~2:35 (~555 words)

### Re-Hook Beat
"Cursor, Lovable, Bolt, Replit Agent -- they all have the same problem. They're middlemen. And middlemen always get squeezed."

Bold claim. Works standalone. Any scrub-arrival immediately gets the thesis.

### SETUP (30-40%, ~50-60s, ~185 words)

- **Beat 1:** The pattern revealed. Every SaaS on the kill list had the same structure: a company sitting between you and the underlying capability, charging a markup for a UI. Semrush sat between you and SEO data. Zapier sat between you and API connections. Premiere sat between you and FFmpeg.
- **Beat 2:** AI coding tools are the SAME pattern. Cursor, Lovable, Bolt -- they sit between you and the AI model. They add a UI. They charge a markup. They're wrappers.

### TENSION (40-50%, ~60-70s, ~220 words)

- **Beat 3:** The Cursor proof. Cursor cut their Pro plan from 500 requests to 225. CEO publicly apologized (June 2025). Why: underlying API costs rose and Cursor absorbed the margin. When you're a wrapper, your economics are controlled by the company you wrap.
- **Beat 4:** The historical pattern. Travel agents sat between you and airlines. Music labels sat between you and artists. Taxi companies sat between you and drivers. Every time the underlying capability becomes directly accessible, the middleman gets squeezed out. Forward pull: "So what's the move?"
- **Beat 5:** Claude Code talks directly to the API. No wrapper. No middleman. No company between you and the capability that can cut your requests in half overnight.

### PAYOFF (10-20%, ~25-30s, ~150 words)

- **Beat 6 (The mindset shift -- core payoff):** Stop asking "which tool should I use?" Start asking: "What's the layer I'm paying for, and can I remove it?" Every subscription is a bet that the vendor can do something you can't. That bet is expiring.

- **Beat 7 (Lead magnet #2):** "If you want to learn which layers to remove first and how to build what replaces them, SaaS Accelerator. $47 a month. Link in the description."

  - **Bridge:** SaaS Accelerator. $47/mo. I'm coaching builders through exactly this transition. Link in the description.

## "SO WHAT" CHAINS

### Chain 1: The $285B Wipeout
- **Point:** $285 billion wiped from software stocks in a single quarter.
- **So what:** Enterprise SaaS pricing models (per-seat) break when AI replaces the humans in those seats.
- **Which means:** SaaS companies can't maintain revenue with fewer users, and can't raise prices on remaining ones.
- **Therefore:** The SaaS model is structurally broken for enterprise -- but demand for underlying capabilities goes elsewhere.

### Chain 2: The Kill List Math
- **Point:** $700-$1,100/month in SaaS replaced by $80/month Claude Code.
- **So what:** $7,400-$12,200/year back in your pocket.
- **Which means:** Breakeven drops. Pricing power increases. You can charge less AND keep more.
- **Therefore:** Every SaaS subscription you eliminate makes your entire business more resilient.

### Chain 3: The Middleman Pattern
- **Point:** Cursor cut Pro from 500 to 225 requests because API costs rose.
- **So what:** When you're a wrapper, your economics are controlled by the company you wrap.
- **Which means:** Every wrapper has a ticking clock. The moment the model provider offers direct access at the same price, the wrapper's value evaporates.
- **Therefore:** Stop evaluating tools by their UI. Evaluate by how many layers sit between you and the capability.

### Chain 4: The Mindset Shift
- **Point:** Every subscription is a bet that the vendor can do something you can't.
- **So what:** That bet made sense when building was hard and required specialized teams.
- **Which means:** As building becomes accessible to individuals, the number of bets worth making shrinks every month.
- **Therefore:** Audit every subscription. Ask: "What's the layer I'm paying for? Can I remove it?"

### Chain 5: SaaS Redistribution
- **Point:** Enterprise SaaS crashes while micro SaaS grows 30% YoY.
- **So what:** Demand for software isn't disappearing. It's being served by different players.
- **Which means:** Solo founders with 70%+ margins capture share that belonged to companies with 500 employees.
- **Therefore:** Best time in history to be a solo SaaS builder -- but only if you're not also dependent on the same SaaS model that's breaking.

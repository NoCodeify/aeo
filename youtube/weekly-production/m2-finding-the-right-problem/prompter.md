So how do you actually test it?

There's a difference between an idea and an observation. An idea is something you think up at a desk. You get excited. You picture the landing page. You start thinking about the name. A problem is something you observe. You watched it happen in real life. Not in your head. Not in a market report. In front of you.

Every project in the four hundred and fifty thousand dollar graveyard started as an idea. Not one of them started from watching someone suffer.

So here's a better way to check. Question one. Can they describe the pain? Not you. Them.

If someone who has the problem can't tell you what's broken in their own words, unprompted, it's not a real problem. They said it. Not you. 'Yeah I guess that would be nice' is a feature request. Not a pain point.

You need someone who says 'this is broken, I've tried three things, none of them work, and it costs me time every single day.' That's pain. That's worth building for.

The drink dispenser. Nobody complained about ordering drinks. Snapchat for business. Nobody described screenshot pain. See the difference? Neither started with someone in pain. They started with someone excited at a desk.

If you can't find someone who describes the pain on their own, you're building for a problem that only exists in your head. But a real problem isn't enough. People have real problems they'd never pay to solve.

Question two. How are they solving it now?

Every real problem has a current solution. Even if it's terrible. A spreadsheet from 2009. A sticky note on someone's monitor. Hiring a person to do it manually. If there's no current solution at all, be skeptical. Nobody cares enough to solve it.

The current solution tells you two things. What you're competing against. And what people already pay. So when you see bad solutions, don't run. That's your opening.

Question three. Would they pay to make it go away?

Not 'would they use it for free.' Would they pay. Monthly. Recurring. 'Maybe' is a no. 'That sounds cool' is a no. 'I'd definitely check it out' is a no.

Better question. When did you last pay for a tool to solve this? Past behavior beats future promises every time. If they've never spent a dollar on this problem, they're probably not going to start with you.

Three questions. Takes five minutes to run on any idea. Most founders never ask even one. They skip straight to building because building feels like progress.

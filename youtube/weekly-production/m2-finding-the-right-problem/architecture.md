## BLOCK 1: "The Filter" (0:28-3:43) ~700 words

**Chapter title:** The 3 Questions Nobody Taught You to Ask
**Re-hook beat:** "Sixty-eight percent of failed SaaS startups built products nobody wanted. Not because they didn't validate. Because they validated the wrong things." (Works for scrub-arrivals: surprising stat, counterintuitive claim, challenges what the viewer thinks validation means.)
**Purpose:** Reveal ALL three questions upfront. Establish the framework. Use the $450K graveyard as anti-proof with brief callbacks (not retelling). The viewer leaves this block knowing all three questions and understanding why they matter.
**Estimated Length:** ~3:15 (~700 words at 217 WPM)

### SETUP (25%, ~175 words)

- **Beat 1 (Re-hook + stat):** "Sixty-eight percent of failed SaaS startups built products nobody wanted. Not because they didn't validate. Because they validated the wrong things." Opens with a stat that reframes the problem. Most people think validation = Googling competitors and checking market size. This beat says: that's not validation, that's reassurance.
- **Beat 2 (Standard playbook is reassurance):** "The standard validation playbook -- Google your competitors, check the market size, sketch some features, start building. That's not validation. That's reassurance. You're not testing whether the idea works. You're looking for permission to build it."
- **Beat 3 (Idea vs. observation):** "There's a difference between an idea and an observation. An idea is something you think up at a desk. A problem is something you OBSERVE. You saw someone struggle with it. You watched it happen." Brief graveyard callback (one sentence, NOT retelling): "Every project in the $450K graveyard started as an idea. Not one of them started from watching someone suffer."

### TENSION (45%, ~315 words)

- **Beat 4 (Q1 REVEAL):** "Question one: Can they describe the pain? Not you. Them. If someone who has the problem can't articulate what's broken in their own words, unprompted, it's not a real problem. 'Yeah I guess that would be nice' is a feature request, not a pain point." Quick graveyard callbacks (one sentence each): "The drink dispenser -- nobody complained about ordering drinks. Snapchat for business -- nobody described screenshot pain. Those were ideas. Not observations."
- **Beat 5 (Forward Pull to Q2):** "But a real problem isn't enough. People have real problems they'd never pay to solve."
- **Beat 6 (Q2 REVEAL):** "Question two: How are they solving it now? Every real problem has a current solution. Even if it's terrible. Even if it's a spreadsheet or a sticky note or hiring someone to do it manually. If there's NO current solution, be skeptical. It might mean nobody cares enough to solve it. The current solution tells you two things: what you're competing against, and what people already pay."
- **Beat 7 (Q3 REVEAL):** "Question three: Would they pay to make it go away? Not 'would they use it for free.' Would they pay. Monthly. Recurring. The word 'maybe' is a no." Q3 upgrade: "Better question: 'When did you last pay for a tool to solve this?' Past behavior beats future promises every time. If they've never spent a dollar on this problem, they're probably not going to start with you."

### PAYOFF (15%, ~105 words)

- **Beat 8 (Framework crystallized):** "Three questions. Takes five minutes to run on any idea. Most founders never ask even one. The graveyard projects failed all three. Not one started with someone describing their pain. Not one checked what people were already paying. Not one asked 'would you pay?'"
- **Beat 9 (The filter metaphor):** "This is a filter, not a brainstorming tool. Ideas go in. Most don't come out. That's the point."

### RETENTION (10%, ~70 words)

- **Beat 10 (Loop close + loop open):** "You have the framework. But frameworks are useless without proof. Let me run a real project through all three questions. One that turned into a hundred thousand dollars a year. And then I'll run one of my current projects through them and be completely honest about where it fails."

### WHY THIS MATTERS
After Beat 4 (Q1 reveal): "If you can't find someone who describes the pain without you leading them there, you're building a solution to a problem that only exists in your head." (Natural, not announced. Woven into the Q1 explanation.)

## BLOCK 2: "The Proof" (3:43-6:43) ~650 words

**Chapter title:** How a $100K SaaS Passed All 3 Questions
**Re-hook beat:** "One product. Three questions. Hundred thousand dollars a year. Let me show you exactly how it scored." (Works for scrub-arrivals: specific revenue, specific framework reference, promise of a concrete walkthrough.)
**Purpose:** Run DM Champ through all three questions as proof the framework works. Not a retelling of the DM Champ story (that was M1). This is the story through a NEW lens: the 3-question filter.
**Estimated Length:** ~3:00 (~650 words at 217 WPM)

### SETUP (30%, ~195 words)

- **Beat 1 (Re-hook):** "One product. Three questions. Hundred thousand dollars a year. Let me show you exactly how it scored."
- **Beat 2 (M1 callback -- brief, NOT retelling):** "You saw DM Champ in the last video. Marketing agency, twenty-plus clinics, leads coming in and dying because nobody replied fast enough. That's the backstory. Now let me run it through the three questions." One sentence of context. Assumes the viewer watched M1. If they didn't, it still works: agency, clinics, leads dying.
- **Beat 3 (Frame):** "This isn't hindsight. I'm not cherry-picking a success story and making the questions fit. The questions work because they force you to look at what's already in front of you. They don't predict success. They filter out obvious failure."
- **Beat 4 (Filter metaphor reference 2):** "So let's put DM Champ through the filter."

### TENSION (40%, ~260 words)

- **Beat 5 (Q1 applied):** "Question one: Can they describe the pain? The clinic owners didn't articulate the problem themselves. They didn't walk up to me and say 'my follow-up is too slow.' I observed it. I watched leads come in across twenty businesses and die because nobody responded in time. The pain was real. It was visible. And it was repeated. That's even stronger than someone describing it -- I saw it happen over and over." Pass. But with a nuance: observation can be stronger than self-report when you see the pattern at scale.
- **Beat 6 (Q2 applied):** "Question two: How are they solving it now? GoHighLevel had an AI chatbot. Other chatbots existed. Every single one opened with 'How can I help you today?' and the lead bounced. Bad alternatives are a green flag, not a red flag. Because the current solutions told me exactly what to beat and proved that people were already spending money in this space." Pass.
- **Beat 7 (Q3 applied):** "Question three: Would they pay to make it go away? The first paying customer came from a cold email. Booked a call. Became a client. They paid because the product answered leads in seconds instead of hours. They were already losing money on missed leads every single day. I didn't ask 'would you pay?' I asked by sending a cold email with a price. And they said yes with their wallet." Pass. Q3 upgrade demonstrated: past behavior (they were already losing money) beat future promises.

### PAYOFF (15%, ~98 words)

- **Beat 8 (Three for three):** "Three for three. And the product was ugly. Barely worked. Five customers held together with duct tape. The product quality didn't matter. What mattered is that all three questions had clear answers before a single line of code was written."
- **Beat 9 (Filter metaphor reference 3):** "The filter doesn't guarantee success. It guarantees you're not building a drink dispenser."

### RETENTION (10%, ~65 words)

- **Beat 10 (Loop close + loop open):** "DM Champ proves the filter works when all three questions pass. But what happens when a project doesn't pass all three? I'm building one right now that fails Question 3. And I'm doing it anyway. Let me show you why."

### WHY THIS MATTERS
After Beat 7 (Q3 applied): "He didn't say 'maybe.' He didn't say 'that sounds cool.' He pulled out his wallet. That's the only validation that counts." (Natural, not announced.)

## BLOCK 3: "The Honest Run" (6:43-9:08) ~520 words

**Chapter title:** I'm Building a SaaS That Fails Question 3
**Re-hook beat:** "I'm about to run my own current project through these three questions right now. And I'm not going to lie about the results." (Works for scrub-arrivals: radical honesty, personal stakes, vulnerability. The word "lie" is a pattern interrupt.)
**Purpose:** Run the Dutch exam app through all 3 questions with radical honesty. Demonstrate the framework being used with integrity. Address "what NOT to build" signals. The viewer sees what a partial pass looks like and how to think about it.
**Estimated Length:** ~2:25 (~520 words at 217 WPM)

### SETUP (30%, ~156 words)

- **Beat 1 (Re-hook):** "I'm about to run my own current project through these three questions right now. And I'm not going to lie about the results."
- **Beat 2 (Dutch exam app context -- brief):** "My wife is British, lives in the Netherlands. She needs to pass the Dutch integration exam. Tried Duolingo -- too generic, doesn't cover the exam format. Looked at language schools -- five thousand a year." M1 callback, compressed to two sentences. Not retelling -- just enough context for the filter to work.
- **Beat 3 (Frame):** "This is a demo project I'm building inside the curriculum. It's not a success story. It's a test case. Let me run it through the filter."

### TENSION (40%, ~208 words)

- **Beat 4 (Q1 applied):** "Question one: Can she describe the pain? Yes. The exam has specific sections -- reading, listening, writing, speaking, KNM. Duolingo doesn't cover any of them. She can tell me exactly what's broken and why. Pass."
- **Beat 5 (Q2 applied):** "Question two: How is she solving it now? Duolingo, which is free but too generic. Or language schools at five thousand a year. Nothing in between. Clear gap. Two bad options on opposite ends. Pass."
- **Beat 6 (Q3 applied -- the honest moment):** "Question three: Would she pay to make it go away? Honest answer -- I haven't validated willingness to pay beyond one person. It's a small market. Dutch integration exam takers in the Netherlands. I'm going through with it because I need it myself and I want to build it as a demo for this curriculum. But 'I think people would pay' is not the same as 'people have paid.' That's a fail."
- **Beat 7 (Why honesty matters more than the answer):** "And that honesty is actually more important than the answer. Most founders lie to themselves at Question 3. They say 'obviously people would pay for this' without ever asking anyone. That's how you end up with a two-hundred-thousand-dollar drink dispenser."
- **Beat 8 (Red flags / "What NOT to build" signals):** "While you're running your ideas through these questions, watch for red flags. Zero competition doesn't mean you found a goldmine. It might mean nobody wants it. Low-margin businesses -- restaurants, small retail -- they barely profit. Good luck selling them SaaS. Local businesses need hand-holding, not a self-serve dashboard. And if there's too much competition, that's a distribution question -- you need to go back to what we covered in the last video."

### PAYOFF (15%, ~78 words)

- **Beat 9 (The framework is about honesty, not yeses):** "The filter isn't about getting three yeses. It's about getting honest answers. An idea that fails Q3 but you build anyway because you need it yourself? Fine. An idea that fails Q1 and you build anyway because you're excited? That's a two-hundred-thousand-dollar drink dispenser." Filter metaphor reference 4.
- **Beat 10 (What the questions tell you):** "The questions don't tell you what to build. They tell you what you're risking."

### RETENTION (10%, ~52 words)

- **Beat 11 (Loop close + loop open into Final Block):** "You now have a filter you can run on any idea in five minutes. Three questions. But knowing whether to build something and knowing how to make money from it are two different problems."

### WHY THIS MATTERS
After Beat 6 (Q3 fail): "'I think people would pay' is the most expensive sentence in SaaS. It's what every founder says right before they spend six months building something nobody buys." (Natural, not announced.)

## FINAL BLOCK (9:08-10:00) ~200 words

**No chapter title.** Flows directly from Block 3's payoff. No transition signal. No ending signals.

### Insight
"You don't need more ideas. You need a filter. Three questions. Can they describe the pain? How are they solving it now? Would they pay to make it go away? Every idea you've ever abandoned could have been tested in five minutes. And every idea you've been excited about deserves an honest answer before you spend a single day building it."

No "wrapping up." No "final thoughts." No "to summarize." This is a statement of what the viewer now understands, delivered as a natural continuation of Block 3's momentum.

### Gap
"So you've found a real problem. People can describe the pain. They're already paying for bad alternatives. They'd pay you to fix it. But how do you actually make money from it? Subscription? Pay-per-use? White labeling? The business model you pick determines everything -- your pricing, your customers, your margins, your life."

Creates a specific need: the viewer now has a validated problem but doesn't know which business model to wrap around it. This gap drives the end screen click.

### Bridge
"The next video covers the business models that work for solo SaaS. Including one that most founders never consider."

Natural transition to M3. Not a pitch. An extension of what they just learned. "One that most founders never consider" is a curiosity gap that drives the click.

## "SO WHAT" CHAINS

**Point 1: "68% of failed SaaS startups built products nobody wanted."**
- So what? -> The default path (build first) has a 68% failure rate baked in.
- Which means -> Validation isn't optional. It's the difference between the 68% and everyone else.
- Therefore -> You need a framework that catches this BEFORE you build, not after.

**Point 2: "Can they describe the pain? If not, it's not a real problem."**
- So what? -> Most founders confuse their own excitement with market demand.
- Which means -> The first filter isn't "is this a good idea?" It's "can someone else describe this pain without you leading them?"
- Therefore -> Stop pitching. Start listening. If you have to explain the problem, it's your idea, not their pain.

**Point 3: "How are they solving it now? Bad alternatives are a green flag."**
- So what? -> Competition isn't the threat. The threat is NO competition.
- Which means -> Existing solutions prove people care enough to spend money AND they show you exactly what to beat.
- Therefore -> When you see bad alternatives, get excited. That's your opening.

**Point 4: "Would they pay? Past behavior beats future promises."**
- So what? -> "Would you use this?" and "would you pay for this?" get different answers.
- Which means -> Free users and paying customers are fundamentally different populations. Only one matters for a business.
- Therefore -> Don't ask "would you pay?" Ask "when did you last pay for a tool to solve this?"

**Point 5: "I'm building a SaaS that fails Question 3."**
- So what? -> Even the person teaching the framework doesn't pass it perfectly.
- Which means -> The framework isn't about perfection. It's about awareness. Knowing WHERE you're taking risk is the insight.
- Therefore -> Run every idea through the filter. Accept the risk with open eyes. Don't lie to yourself at Q3.

## DEEPER PROBLEM

**Surface:** Which launch channel should I pick to get my first SaaS customers?
**Deeper:** Dabbling -- trying a little bit of everything, never committing to one channel. The emotional barrier isn't a knowledge gap. It's that picking one channel means accepting the other seven won't save you. Dabbling is comfortable because you always have another channel to try next.

**Plant (Block 1, end):** "I tried everything once. A little bit of Product Hunt, a little bit of AppSumo, a little bit of ads, a little bit of LinkedIn. Sound familiar?" -- Natural, observational. Names the behavior but doesn't label it as the problem. Feels like a passing admission.

**Thread (Block 3, after cold email):** "Always moving, never going deep. And six months later they've tried eight channels and committed to zero." -- Names the behavior pattern explicitly. Viewer recognizes themselves. Still framed as something "people" do, not a direct accusation.

**Payoff (Block 4, near end):** "The reason most founders try everything and commit to nothing isn't because they can't pick. It's because picking one channel means accepting that the other seven won't save you. And that's scary. Because if you go all in on content and it doesn't work, you've got nothing to blame except yourself. Dabbling is comfortable because you always have another channel to try next." -- Explicitly names the deeper problem and the emotional mechanism behind it.

## BLOCK 1: "The Overhyped Ones" (#8 Product Hunt + #7 AppSumo)

**Purpose:** Dispatch the two channels most commonly recommended by gurus and internet advice. Establish early that popular doesn't mean effective. Sets the tone for the whole video: real experience over conventional wisdom.
**Estimated Length:** ~2:30 (~500 words)

### SETUP (25-35%)

- Beat 1 (Re-hook): "Two channels. Both popular. Both recommended by everyone you follow. Both nearly useless for getting your first paying customers." -- Bold claim for scrub-arrivals. Works standalone.
- Beat 2 (Product Hunt framing): Product Hunt is the default recommendation. Every "how to launch your SaaS" guide says to do it. Every indie hacker forum says to do it.
- Beat 3 (Product Hunt reality): The actual experience -- a backlink and basically nothing else. The audience on Product Hunt is other founders, not buyers. They're browsing, not purchasing. Only half of founders see any increase in registrations after launching.
- Beat 4 (Problem statement): Product Hunt is a vanity launch. It feels like a launch because you get upvotes and comments. But upvotes don't pay the bills. It's a backlink generator, not a customer acquisition channel.

### TENSION (35-45%)

- Beat 5 (AppSumo pivot): "So I tried the next thing everyone recommends. AppSumo."
- Beat 6 (AppSumo numbers): Real numbers -- ~$20K gross revenue. Sounds great. Then the refund rate: 40%. Net after refunds: ~$15K. 300 users down to ~200 after refunds. All lifetime deal. They paid once and never again.
- Beat 7 (The lifetime deal trap): Those 200 users are on your support queue forever. They paid $49 once. They'll never upgrade. They'll never churn because there's nothing to churn from. You gave away your product for a one-time payment.
- Beat 8 (AppSumo now): Platform has gotten really strict. Harder to get listed. Channel might be dead for new SaaS products.
- Beat 9 (Complication): "And the money wasn't even the real problem."
- Beat 10 (Discovery -- DEEPER PROBLEM PLANT): "I know because I tried both. And I didn't just try them once. I tried everything once. A little bit of Product Hunt, a little bit of AppSumo, a little bit of ads, a little bit of LinkedIn. Sound familiar?" -- First mention of dabbling. Planted as a passing admission, not labeled as the deeper problem.

### PAYOFF (10-15%)

- Beat 11 (Revelation): Two most recommended channels. One gave a backlink. One gave $15K net and 200 lifetime users who'll never pay again. Neither gave a sustainable customer acquisition path.
- Beat 12 (Action step): If you haven't launched yet, skip both. If you're already on AppSumo, understand the math: lifetime users have a ceiling.

### RETENTION (5-10%)

- Beat 13 (Loop close + new loop): "Two channels down. Both popular. Both nearly useless. But the next two are worse in a different way. They actually work. Just not when you're starting out. And the reason has nothing to do with the channels themselves." -- Closes "are popular channels good?" loop. Opens "what about channels that work but not now?" loop.

### WHY THIS MATTERS

- After Beat 4: "Upvotes feel like customers. They're not. And the gap between feeling like you launched and actually acquiring a customer is where most SaaS founders get stuck for months." (Natural, not announced.)

### CREATIVE ELEMENTS

- Visual demo: Excalidraw slide showing Product Hunt upvotes vs. actual customers (big number vs. zero). Second slide showing AppSumo math: $20K gross -> 40% refund -> $15K net -> 200 lifetime users.
- Story: Product Hunt launch (personal, real). AppSumo results (personal, real numbers).
- Metaphor: Product Hunt as a "vanity launch" -- feels like a launch, looks like a launch, isn't a launch.

### TENSION LOOP

- Raise: "Both popular. Both recommended." (expectation: they must work)
- Tease: Product Hunt reality (backlink, nothing else)
- Deepen: AppSumo numbers look good on the surface ($20K)
- Release: The refund math ($15K net, 200 lifetime users, channel getting stricter)
- Re-tension: "I tried everything once" + forward pull to next two channels

## BLOCK 2: "The Money Pits" (#6 Paid Ads + #5 SEO)

**Purpose:** These aren't bad channels -- they're wrong-stage channels. Paid ads require capital and expertise you don't have yet. SEO requires time and has broken attribution. Neither is where your FIRST customer comes from. Establish the pattern: everything so far either costs too much money, takes too long, or can't prove ROI.
**Estimated Length:** ~2:30 (~500 words)

### SETUP (25-35%)

- Beat 1 (Re-hook): "Twenty euros per click. For people searching for the cheapest option. That was my Google Ads experience." -- Specific number, specific frustration. Works for scrub-arrivals.
- Beat 2 (Paid ads framing): Paid ads sound logical. You pay money, you get customers. Simple.
- Beat 3 (Problem statement): It's not simple. It's an entire operation masquerading as a channel.

### TENSION (35-45%)

- Beat 4 (Google Ads specifics): EUR 20/click. And the people clicking? They're searching for the cheapest option. They're comparison shopping. You're paying premium prices to reach bargain hunters.
- Beat 5 (Meta Ads specifics): Tons of experience with Meta. What makes it hard isn't the platform. It's the ecosystem. You need to know how to write the ads, the creative, the landing page, the follow-up sequence. Each one is its own skill. It's a full-time job.
- Beat 6 (Data): SaaS CPC increased for 87% of industries in 2025. Getting more expensive, not less. And you're competing against companies with dedicated marketing teams and six-figure ad budgets.
- Beat 7 (The real issue): You're paying to acquire customers before you know your unit economics. Before you know your churn rate. Before you know your lifetime value. You're guessing with money you don't have.
- Beat 8 (SEO pivot): "So what about the free version of this? SEO."
- Beat 9 (SEO reality): "The thing is you can't even really track it. It's traffic, but how do you know that specific traffic leads to a sale?" -- Direct quote from user's experience. SEO attribution is broken. AI is making it worse -- zero-click results, AI overviews eating clicks. Traditional analytics can't tell you which visit became a customer.
- Beat 10 (SEO timing): Even if attribution worked, SEO takes 6-12 months to show results. Your first customer needs to come faster than that.

### PAYOFF (10-15%)

- Beat 11 (ASK-DON'T-TELL #1): "Four channels down. Product Hunt, AppSumo, paid ads, SEO. Not one of them got me my first paying customer. You probably already see why." -- Let the viewer identify the pattern: all four either cost money upfront, take months, or can't prove ROI. Don't spell it out.
- Beat 12 (Revelation confirmed within 30s): The pattern is confirmed at the start of Block 3: "Everything above costs money or time you don't have. Everything below costs effort. And when you're starting out, effort is the one thing you actually have."

### RETENTION (5-10%)

- Beat 13 (Loop close + new loop): "Four channels that sound smart but don't work at zero. The next three are the opposite. They're not sexy. Nobody's making YouTube videos telling you to do them. But they cost nothing. And they actually convert." -- Closes the "do expensive channels work?" loop. Opens the "what actually works for free?" loop.

### WHY THIS MATTERS

- After Beat 7: "Every dollar you spend on ads before you understand your numbers is a dollar you're gambling, not investing." (Natural, not announced.)

### CREATIVE ELEMENTS

- Visual demo: Excalidraw slide showing Paid Ads equation: EUR 20/click x 100 clicks = EUR 2,000 spent. Conversion rate 2% = 2 customers. CPA = EUR 1,000/customer. Below: "Do you even know if a customer is worth EUR 1,000?" Second visual: SEO timeline showing 6-12 month lag with question mark at the end ("Did it work? Can't tell.").
- Story: Personal Google Ads experience (EUR 20/click, price shoppers). Personal Meta Ads experience (full-time job, ecosystem of skills).
- Metaphor: Paid ads as "gambling before you know the rules" -- you're placing bets before you understand the odds.

### TENSION LOOP

- Raise: "Twenty euros per click" (immediate shock -- that's expensive)
- Tease: Meta Ads -- tons of experience, but it's a whole ecosystem
- Deepen: CPC up 87%, competing against teams with six-figure budgets
- Release: SEO is "free" but takes 6-12 months and you can't track it
- Re-tension: "Four channels. Zero first customers. You probably see why." + forward pull to the channels that do work

## BLOCK 3: "The Ones That Actually Work" (#4 Affiliate + #3 LinkedIn + #2 Cold Email)

**Purpose:** Shift from channels that drain resources to channels that cost nothing and just require effort. This is where the video gets actionable. Three channels that are free, direct, and measurable. None of them are sexy. That's the point.
**Estimated Length:** ~3:00 (~650 words)

### SETUP (25-35%)

- Beat 1 (Re-hook): "Everything above costs money or time you don't have. Everything below costs effort. And when you're starting out, effort is the one thing you actually have." -- Clean split. Works for scrub-arrivals. Confirms the ask-don't-tell pattern from Block 2.
- Beat 2 (Context): Three channels left. All free. All require effort instead of money. None of them get recommended in the typical "how to launch" guides.
- Beat 3 (Affiliate intro -- #4): "Starting with the easiest one. Affiliate programs."

### TENSION (35-45%)

- Beat 4 (#4 Affiliate Program): Not a channel. A mechanism. Set it up and forget it. Takes 30 minutes. Costs nothing. Just means someone else gets a cut when they refer a customer to you. Not your main strategy. A passive layer that sits alongside whatever you're actually doing. You'd be stupid not to have it running.
- Beat 5 (#3 LinkedIn -- M4 callback): "I covered LinkedIn in the last video. It works. Ten to twenty-five percent reply rates compared to one to five percent on cold email. If you haven't set up your profile yet, go watch that video." -- One sentence. Done. This is a callback, not a deep dive. M4 covered LinkedIn in detail.
- Beat 6 (#2 Cold Email -- M1 callback + expansion): "DM Champ's first paying customer came from a cold email. I covered that story already." -- M1 callback in one sentence.
- Beat 7 (Cold email mechanics): What I didn't cover is why cold email works even when the numbers look terrible. Three to four percent reply rate. That means you send a hundred emails and three or four people respond. Sounds brutal. But those three or four people are real conversations with real potential buyers. Not impressions. Not clicks. Conversations.
- Beat 8 (Cold email vs. everything above): Product Hunt gives you upvotes from founders. Ads give you clicks from price shoppers. SEO gives you traffic you can't track. Cold email gives you a conversation with someone who read your message and chose to respond. That's a different quality of lead entirely.
- Beat 9 (DEEPER PROBLEM THREAD): "Notice how none of these are revolutionary? Setting up an affiliate program. Sending cold emails. Posting on LinkedIn. None of this is sexy. And that's exactly why people don't do it. They'd rather try a little bit of everything. Product Hunt one week, AppSumo the next, maybe some ads, maybe some SEO. Always moving, never going deep. And six months later they've tried eight channels and committed to zero."
- Beat 10 (ASK-DON'T-TELL #2): "Every successful SaaS founder I know picked one of these and went all in. Zero of them tried all eight. You probably already know which one you should pick. You're just not doing it yet."

### PAYOFF (10-15%)

- Beat 11 (Revelation): Three free channels. All work. Affiliate is passive. LinkedIn is covered. Cold email is direct and measurable. But none of them compound. They work as long as you're actively doing them. Stop sending emails, stop getting customers.
- Beat 12 (Forward pull): "There's one channel left. The one I ranked number one. And it's different from everything else on this list because it doesn't stop working when you stop."

### RETENTION (5-10%)

- Beat 13 (Loop close + new loop): Closes "what works for free?" loop. Opens "what compounds?" loop. The viewer now knows cold email works, but wants to know what beats it.

### WHY THIS MATTERS

- After Beat 8: "The difference between a click and a conversation is the difference between a metric and a customer. Most founders optimize for metrics because metrics feel like progress." (Natural, not announced.)

### CREATIVE ELEMENTS

- Visual demo: Excalidraw slide showing the three channels side by side. Affiliate: "Set up once, passive." LinkedIn: "10-25% reply rate (see M4)." Cold Email: "3-4% reply rate, but real conversations." Second visual: comparison table of all 8 channels showing what each one actually gives you (upvotes vs. clicks vs. traffic vs. conversations).
- Story: DM Champ cold email origin (M1 callback, one sentence). No new full story -- this block is about the pattern, not individual narratives.
- Metaphor: Channels that "stop when you stop" vs. the one that compounds.

### TENSION LOOP

- Raise: "Everything below costs effort" (expectation: work, not magic)
- Tease: Affiliate -- too easy, barely a channel. LinkedIn -- one sentence callback.
- Deepen: Cold email -- numbers look terrible (3-4%) but conversations > clicks
- Release: "You probably already know which one to pick"
- Re-tension: "One channel left. It doesn't stop when you stop." + forward pull to #1

## BLOCK 4: "The One That Compounds" (#1 Organic Content)

**Purpose:** Reveal the winner. Organic content compounds -- every post is a permanent asset. It's free. It's one-to-many. And it's the channel most founders skip because it feels slow. This is the payoff the hook promised ("the one that actually worked is not the one you'd expect").
**Estimated Length:** ~2:30 (~500 words)

### SETUP (25-35%)

- Beat 1 (Re-hook): "A company called Cluely hit a hundred thousand dollars in monthly revenue without spending a dollar on ads. All organic. All content. All free." -- External proof. Specific number. Works for scrub-arrivals.
- Beat 2 (Context): No ad budget. No sales team. No outbound. Just content that went viral and kept compounding.
- Beat 3 (Why this is #1): "Every other channel on this list stops working when you stop. Ads stop when you stop paying. Cold email stops when you stop sending. SEO takes months and you can't track it. Content is different."

### TENSION (35-45%)

- Beat 4 (The compounding effect): Every piece of content you create is a permanent asset. A LinkedIn post from six months ago still gets views. A YouTube video from a year ago still gets clicks. A Twitter thread from last quarter still gets shared. Nothing else on this list does that.
- Beat 5 (One-to-many vs. one-to-one): Cold email is one-to-one. You send a hundred emails, you get four conversations. Content is one-to-many. You create one piece of content and a thousand people can see it. The math is completely different.
- Beat 6 (DM Champ's inflection): "DM Champ's real growth didn't come from cold email. Cold email got me the first customer. But the inflection point -- the moment it went from a handful of users to actual traction -- came from content. Posting about what I was building. Showing the product in action. Documenting the journey."
- Beat 7 (Why people skip it): "But here's why most founders never get here. It takes six to twelve months to see results. You post for weeks and nothing happens. No likes. No comments. No leads. And you start thinking maybe you should try ads instead. Or AppSumo. Or Product Hunt. Something that gives you a number today."
- Beat 8 (ASK-DON'T-TELL #3): "Six months of posting with nothing to show for it. And then one post hits. And then another. And then people start reaching out to you instead of you reaching out to them. You probably already see why dabblers never get here."
- Beat 9 (DEEPER PROBLEM PAYOFF): "The reason most founders try everything and commit to nothing isn't because they can't pick. It's because picking one channel means accepting that the other seven won't save you. And that's scary. Because if you go all in on content and it doesn't work, you've got nothing to blame except yourself. Dabbling is comfortable because you always have another channel to try next."

### PAYOFF (10-15%)

- Beat 10 (Revelation): Organic content is the winner. It's free. It compounds. It's one-to-many. And it's the one channel that turns you from someone chasing customers into someone customers come to. But it only works if you commit. Pick one platform. Post consistently. Give it six months before you judge it.
- Beat 11 (Action step): "Pick one platform. Not three. One. Post about what you're building, what you're learning, what's breaking. Do it for six months. That's it."

### RETENTION (5-10%)

- Beat 12 (Transition to Final Block): "The channel is the easy part. What you actually do after you pick it -- the first ninety days, the posting schedule, the mistakes that kill momentum -- that's where most people fall apart."

### WHY THIS MATTERS

- After Beat 5: "The difference between one-to-one and one-to-many isn't just efficiency. It's leverage. Cold email scales linearly. Content scales exponentially. And at zero budget, leverage is the only advantage you have." (Natural, not announced.)

### CREATIVE ELEMENTS

- Visual demo: Excalidraw slide showing compounding curve. X-axis: months (0-12). Y-axis: audience/leads. Flat line for months 1-6, then exponential curve months 7-12. Label: "This is why people quit at month 4." Second visual: comparison -- cold email (linear line) vs. content (exponential curve).
- Story: Cluely -- $100K+ MRR from organic virality. DM Champ inflection point from content (personal, brief).
- Metaphor: Content as "compound interest for attention" -- each piece builds on the last.

### TENSION LOOP

- Raise: Cluely -- $100K+ MRR, no ads (sounds too good)
- Tease: Every piece of content is a permanent asset (the mechanism)
- Deepen: It takes 6-12 months. Most quit at month 4.
- Release: The deeper problem payoff -- dabbling is comfortable because you always have another channel to try
- Re-tension: "Pick one. Six months. That's it." + forward pull: what happens in those six months?

## FINAL BLOCK

**Insight:** "Eight channels. Most of them don't work for your first customers. Product Hunt gives you a backlink. AppSumo gives you lifetime users who never pay again. Paid ads cost too much before you know your numbers. SEO takes too long and you can't track it. Affiliate sits in the background. LinkedIn and cold email work but stop when you stop. Organic content is the only one that compounds."

**Identity Shift:** "Even now, when I have a new project, my first instinct is to test three channels at once. Not to pick one and commit. The dabbling instinct doesn't go away. You just get better at noticing when you're doing it."

**Bridge to M6:** "The first ninety days after you launch are where most SaaS products die. Not from the product. Not from the market. From the founder losing momentum. What you do in those ninety days, and the one mistake that kills more SaaS products than anything else, is exactly what the next video covers."

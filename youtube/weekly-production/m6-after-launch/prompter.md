The silence is normal. Five days, five weeks, sometimes five months. It happened to me. It'll happen to you. Every founder I've talked to went through some version of this. The question isn't whether the silence happens. It happens to everyone. The question is what you do during it.

And what most people do during the silence is exactly the wrong thing. They start building. Adding features. Making the product 'better.' Feature creep is a response to panic, not a response to data. Nobody emailed you asking for a Google Calendar import. Nobody said 'I'd pay if only you had this one feature.' You're not building what users asked for. You're building what makes you feel less anxious about the silence.

And while you're busy building features nobody needs, something else is happening with your actual customers. Something worse.

Wrong customers. People who were never going to succeed with your product no matter what you built.

One of our biggest churn drivers at DM Champ was people signing up for a chatbot without website traffic. Think about that for a second. A chatbot without traffic is like a salesperson in an empty store. The tool works. There's just nobody there to use it.

Two failure modes. Building things nobody asked for, like the Google Calendar import. And acquiring customers who can't succeed with your product, like the chatbot with no traffic. Both feel productive. Neither moves you forward.

The silence isn't a product problem. Adding more features won't fix it. Getting more users who aren't ready for your product won't fix it either. The silence is a distribution and fit problem. You don't need a better product. You need the right people to actually find the product you already have.

See the gap? You've got two failure modes that feel productive and one actual problem nobody wants to face. Distribution and fit.

So if building features is wrong and chasing any customer is wrong, what do you actually do during the silence? Because the answer isn't exciting. It's not a hack. It's not a shortcut. It's not something you can do in a weekend. It's something most founders refuse to commit to for long enough.


It takes about ninety days to get good at one channel. Not to master it. Not to go viral. Just to stop being terrible at it.

That's not a marketing theory. It's just the reality of learning anything new. You're bad at it for weeks. Then slightly less bad. Then decent enough that the numbers start moving. And those first weeks of being bad are the part that kills most people because it feels like nothing is working.

Cold email. If you're getting at least one percent reply rate, keep going. If you're getting zero, something's wrong with the message, not the market. LinkedIn. I covered the specific numbers in a previous video. Those are your benchmarks. They're not exciting numbers. But they're real.

Pick one. Not two. Not three. One channel. And give it a real ninety-day shot before you judge whether it works.

And while you're doing that, your pricing is going to change. A lot. More than you think.

With DM Champ, we changed the pricing constantly. Started with pay-per-result because that was the easy sell. Then pay-per-usage once we had a real product. Then we switched to subscriptions once we actually understood our costs and margins. Then adjusted the subscription price. And again. And again.

No grand strategy behind any of it. No pricing consultant. No spreadsheet model that predicted the perfect price point. Just looking at how the market responds and adjusting. We'd raise the price and see if anyone pushed back. Sometimes they did. Sometimes they didn't even notice. You see what competitors charge. You see where people drop off. And you respond. The pricing never really settles. Even now we still adjust it. You just keep tuning.

Changing onboarding, adjusting pricing, tweaking the landing page -- that's the actual work. Not one big pivot. Not one genius insight. Not one conversation that changes everything. Small adjustments, week after week, for months. Testing different price points. Rewriting how you describe the product. Shortening the free trial. Lengthening it again. Nobody posts about any of this on Twitter. Nobody makes a YouTube video about 'I changed my pricing for the fourth time and it might be slightly better now.' But that's the actual job after you launch.

And iteration only works if you picked one thing to iterate on. You can't iterate on five channels at once. You can't test three pricing models simultaneously. Pick one. Measure. Adjust. Repeat. For ninety days. I covered in the last video why trying everything at once kills your momentum.

That's the grind. It's not glamorous. Nobody's going to congratulate you for changing your onboarding email for the sixth time. But it's the only thing that actually works.

## BLOCK 1: "The Reference Check" (0:22 - 3:45) ~720 words

**Chapter title:** They Googled You Before You Even Knew
**Re-hook beat:** "A business spending a thousand dollars a month did a three-person reference check before hiring me. A thousand dollars. Three references. One of them was a clinic in Miami." (Works cold for scrub-arrivals: specific number, specific detail, surprising disproportionality.)
**Purpose:** Shatter the belief that "the product should speak for itself." Prove that buyers investigate you thoroughly even at tiny deal sizes. Establish that professional presentation isn't vanity -- it's surviving due diligence.
**Estimated Length:** ~3:20 (~720 words)

### SETUP (30%, ~215 words)

- **Beat 1 (Re-hook):** Hair clinic story opening. "When I was doing marketing for hair clinics, I had one clinic as a client. Total deal: two thousand dollars. A thousand for me, a thousand for ad spend."
- **Beat 2 (The due diligence reveal):** "Before they signed, they did a full reference check. Three of my past clients. They called each one. One of them was a clinic in Miami. For a thousand dollars a month."
- **Beat 3 (The scale contrast):** "This wasn't a hundred-thousand-dollar enterprise contract. This was a grand a month. And they still checked three references, verified my work, made sure I was legitimate."

### TENSION (45%, ~325 words)

- **Beat 4 (Ask, don't tell #1 -- what this means for the viewer):** "Now imagine showing up to that conversation with a default LinkedIn banner, no website, and no proof you've done this before. What do you think happens?" Viewer draws the conclusion. "You don't even get the conversation. You get skipped. Silently. Nobody tells you why."
- **Beat 5 (The invisible comparison):** "When a buyer is evaluating you, they're comparing you to other options. And the only thing they can judge before buying is how you present yourself. They can't see your code. They can't test your product. They can't evaluate your architecture. All they have is what shows up when they google your name, check your LinkedIn, and look at your landing page."
- **Beat 6 (Deeper problem plant):** "Most developers skip this entirely. Not because they don't know. Because it feels like marketing. And developers don't do marketing. The product should speak for itself." -- Pause. "But the product doesn't get a chance to speak if nobody trusts you enough to try it."
- **Beat 7 (The DM Champ proof):** "When I started selling DM Champ, I had zero paying customers. But I had a good landing page. I had beta testimonials. I had screenshots of the product working. That was it. No revenue numbers. No 'trusted by 500 companies.' Just enough to look real. And that was enough to start getting calls."

### PAYOFF (15%, ~110 words)

- **Beat 8 (The principle):** "Professional presentation isn't about looking fancy. It's about surviving due diligence. People google you. People check your LinkedIn. People look at your landing page. And they do this BEFORE they talk to you. Not after."
- **Beat 9 (Why this matters -- natural, not announced):** After Beat 8: "If you fail the google test, you never get the meeting. You never get the reference check. You never get the chance to show your product. The sale died before it started and you don't even know it."

### RETENTION (10%, ~70 words)

- **Beat 10 (Forward pull to Block 2):** "So the first thing people check is your LinkedIn. And most developer profiles look like they were created to satisfy an HR department in 2019. Let me show you what mine actually looks like and exactly what to change."

### TENSION LOOP
- **Raise:** Beat 1-2 -- "$1K client did a 3-person reference check" (viewer thinks: what would they find if they checked ME?)
- **Tease:** Beat 4 -- "What do you think happens?" (viewer imagines their own empty profile)
- **Deepen:** Beat 5 -- The invisible comparison. They can't see your code. All they see is your online presence.
- **Release:** Beat 8 -- "It's about surviving due diligence."
- **Re-tension:** Beat 10 -- "Most developer profiles look like 2019 HR compliance." (New loop: what's wrong with MY profile?)

### CREATIVE ELEMENTS
- **Visual demo:** SCREEN SHARE of a generic/bad LinkedIn profile (could be a mocked-up example or blurred real one) showing: default banner, "Software Developer at X" headline, empty featured section.
- **Story:** Hair clinic reference check -- contrast ($1K deal, 3-person check), specific detail (Miami clinic reference), visual moment (the disproportionate thoroughness).
- **Metaphor option:** Due diligence wall -- first mention. "There's a wall between you and every buyer. It's called Google. And right now, what's behind that wall is empty."
- **Slide:** Excalidraw "The Google Test" -- what buyers see vs. what you think they see. Left: "What You Think: They see your product." Right: "What Actually Happens: They see your LinkedIn, your landing page, your social proof. Then they decide." Layout: slide_full, 16:9.
- **Audio-friendly:** The hair clinic story works entirely through dialogue. No visual dependency.

## BLOCK 2: "The LinkedIn Fix" (3:45 - 7:15) ~760 words

**Chapter title:** Your LinkedIn Is Costing You Deals Right Now
**Re-hook beat:** "Two hundred connections. Three calls in the first two weeks. I wasn't even trying. The profile was doing the work." (Works cold: specific numbers, surprising result, implies a method.)
**Purpose:** Walk through the founder's actual LinkedIn profile. Show what a SaaS founder's profile should look like vs. a developer resume. Make it immediately actionable -- viewer could fix theirs during the video.
**Estimated Length:** ~3:30 (~760 words)

### SETUP (25%, ~190 words)

- **Beat 1 (Re-hook + story):** "When I set up my LinkedIn properly for DM Champ, I had two hundred connections. Within the first couple of weeks, three people reached out to book calls. I wasn't trying hard. The profile was doing the work."
- **Beat 2 (Ask, don't tell #2):** "Three calls from two hundred connections in the first couple weeks. And I wasn't trying hard. You tell me what changed." Viewer connects the profile to the result.
- **Beat 3 (The contrast):** "Compare that to what most developer profiles look like. 'Software Developer at [Company].' Default banner. Bio that reads like a resume from 2019. Nobody is reaching out to that person to buy their SaaS."
- **Beat 4 (Personal profiles vs company pages):** "Your personal LinkedIn profile gets sixty-five percent of all feed visibility. Company pages get five percent. Your personal profile IS your marketing channel. And right now it's working against you."

### TENSION (45%, ~340 words)

- **Beat 5 (Screen share -- actual LinkedIn walkthrough):** "Let me show you what mine looks like." Screen share of the founder's actual LinkedIn profile. Walk through each element as it appears on screen:
  - **Banner:** Not default. A simple branded banner that communicates what you do. Took 10 minutes to make.
  - **Headline:** Not "Software Developer" -- communicates what problem you solve and for whom. Before/after example on screen.
  - **About section:** Not a resume. A brief story of the problem you solve and why you built the product. Written like a human, not a job application.
  - **Featured section:** This is where your landing page link, a product screenshot, or a short demo video lives. Most developers have nothing here. This is prime real estate.
- **Beat 6 (The common mistakes):** "Most developers treat LinkedIn like a resume. Job title. Company name. Skills list. That's what LinkedIn was in 2015. In 2026, it's where your buyers decide if you're worth talking to. And they decide in about five seconds."
- **Beat 7 (Deeper problem thread):** "This is the part that feels cringey. Writing a LinkedIn headline about what you solve instead of what you are. It feels like you're turning into one of those people. 'Growth hacker.' 'Thought leader.' I get it. But the three calls didn't come from a resume headline. They came from a headline that made someone curious enough to click."
- **Beat 8 (LinkedIn cold outreach stat):** "LinkedIn cold outreach gets ten to twenty-five percent reply rates. Cold email gets one to five percent. The platform is built for this. But only if your profile gives people a reason to reply."

### PAYOFF (15%, ~115 words)

- **Beat 9 (Summary -- actionable checklist):** "Four things to fix right now. Headline: what you solve, not what you are. Banner: branded, not default. About section: the problem, the product, the proof. Featured section: landing page link and a product screenshot. That's it. Takes an afternoon. Most developers never do it because it feels like marketing."
- **Beat 10 (Why this matters -- natural):** After Beat 9: "The difference between 'Software Developer' and a headline that communicates value is the difference between getting skipped and getting a call. Both profiles have the same product behind them. One gets meetings."

### RETENTION (10%, ~75 words)

- **Beat 11 (Forward pull to Block 3):** "LinkedIn gets people to your door. But the door has to open into something real. When someone clicks your landing page link, what do they see? Because there's a specific moment where most SaaS landing pages lose the visitor. And it's not where you think."

### TENSION LOOP
- **Raise:** Beat 1 -- "3 calls from 200 connections, wasn't trying" (viewer thinks: I have more connections and get zero calls)
- **Tease:** Beat 3 -- "Nobody is reaching out to that person" (viewer sees themselves)
- **Deepen:** Beat 5 -- Screen share walkthrough. Each element shows the gap between what the viewer has and what works.
- **Release:** Beat 9 -- "Four things to fix right now." Clear, actionable.
- **Re-tension:** Beat 11 -- "There's a specific moment where most landing pages lose the visitor." (New loop opens.)

### CREATIVE ELEMENTS
- **Visual demo:** SCREEN SHARE of the founder's actual LinkedIn profile. This IS the visual for the entire block. Walk through banner, headline, about, featured section on camera.
- **Slide 1:** Excalidraw "Before/After Headline" -- Left: "Software Developer at [Company]" (red X). Right: "[What you solve] for [who]" (green check). Layout: slide_full, 16:9.
- **Slide 2:** Excalidraw "LinkedIn Profile Checklist" -- four items with checkboxes: Headline, Banner, About, Featured. Clean, hand-drawn. Layout: slide_full, 16:9.
- **Text overlay:** "65% vs 5%" -- on speaker during Beat 4 (personal vs company page reach).
- **Text overlay:** "10-25% vs 1-5%" -- on speaker during Beat 8 (LinkedIn vs email reply rates).
- **Audio-friendly:** Screen share moments need verbal description: "Right now you're looking at my banner -- it's just the logo, the product name, and a one-line description." Audio-only listeners get the information without the visual.

## BLOCK 3: "The Landing Page" (7:15 - 10:30) ~710 words

**Chapter title:** The One Mistake That Kills Every SaaS Landing Page
**Re-hook beat:** "Your landing page doesn't need to be beautiful. It needs to be real. And there's a difference that costs most founders every visitor they get." (Works cold: counterintuitive claim, implies a reveal.)
**Purpose:** Show what a credible SaaS landing page looks like at zero customers. Not design advice -- credibility architecture. Walk through the DM Champ landing page as proof.
**Estimated Length:** ~3:15 (~710 words)

### SETUP (30%, ~215 words)

- **Beat 1 (Re-hook + reframe):** "Your landing page doesn't need to be beautiful. It needs to be real. Most founders get this backwards. They spend weeks on design, animations, gradients, custom illustrations. And then they launch with no social proof, no screenshots, and a value proposition that could apply to any product on earth."
- **Beat 2 (The mistake):** "The one thing that kills landing pages isn't bad design. It's vagueness. When someone lands on your page and can't figure out what you do, who it's for, and whether anyone else uses it within five seconds, they leave. Not because it looks bad. Because it doesn't feel real."
- **Beat 3 (What 'real' means):** "A real landing page answers three questions above the fold. What does this do? Who is it for? Does anyone else actually use it? If your landing page answers all three, you're ahead of most SaaS products. If it answers zero, you look like a side project someone abandoned."

### TENSION (45%, ~320 words)

- **Beat 4 (Screen share -- DM Champ landing page walkthrough):** "Let me show you what mine looks like." Screen share of actual DM Champ landing page. Walk through:
  - **Above the fold:** Value proposition. One sentence that says what DM Champ does and who it's for. Not clever. Clear.
  - **Product screenshots:** Actual screenshots of the dashboard, the conversations, the booking flow. Proves the product exists and works.
  - **Social proof section:** Where testimonials, logos, or usage numbers live. At launch this was beta testimonials and screenshots only.
  - **Call to action:** Clear, single CTA. Not three different buttons going to three different places.
- **Beat 5 (The costly mistake most founders make):** "Most founders look at their landing page and think 'I need better design.' Wrong. They need better specificity. 'AI-powered automation for your business' tells me nothing. 'We reply to your leads on WhatsApp in under 60 seconds so you never lose another booking' tells me everything."
- **Beat 6 (Template trap):** "Templates are fine as a starting point. But if you launch with a template and don't change the stock photos, the generic copy, and the placeholder testimonials, you look exactly like what you are: someone who spent twenty minutes on this and moved on. A hair clinic spending a grand a month will see through that in seconds."
- **Beat 7 (Why this matters -- natural):** After Beat 6: "The landing page isn't where you sell the product. It's where you prove the product is real. That's a lower bar than most people think. But most people still don't clear it."

### PAYOFF (15%, ~105 words)

- **Beat 8 (The landing page minimum):** "Clear value proposition above the fold. Product screenshots. At least one piece of social proof. A single call to action. That's the bar. Not a masterpiece. Not a design award. Just enough that when someone lands on your page, they believe a real person built a real thing."

### RETENTION (10%, ~70 words)

- **Beat 9 (Forward pull to Block 4):** "But here's where most founders get stuck. 'I don't have testimonials. I don't have case studies. I have zero customers.' And they're right. So they launch with nothing. No proof at all. There's a way around this. And it's what I did with DM Champ before a single person had paid me."

### TENSION LOOP
- **Raise:** Beat 1 -- "Doesn't need to be beautiful. Needs to be real." (Viewer thinks: is mine real?)
- **Tease:** Beat 2 -- "Vagueness kills landing pages." (Viewer mentally audits their own copy)
- **Deepen:** Beat 4 -- Screen share walkthrough. Viewer compares their page to DM Champ's.
- **Release:** Beat 8 -- "Clear value prop, screenshots, social proof, CTA. That's the bar."
- **Re-tension:** Beat 9 -- "I have zero customers. No proof at all." (New loop: how do you get proof from nothing?)

### CREATIVE ELEMENTS
- **Visual demo:** SCREEN SHARE of the actual DM Champ landing page. This IS the primary visual for the block.
- **Slide 1:** Excalidraw "The 5-Second Test" -- three questions visitors ask: "What does this do? / Who is it for? / Does anyone use it?" With timer icon. Layout: slide_full, 16:9.
- **Slide 2:** Excalidraw "Vague vs. Specific" -- Left: "AI-powered automation for your business" (red X). Right: "We reply to your leads on WhatsApp in under 60 seconds" (green check). Layout: slide_full, 16:9.
- **Text overlay:** "5 SECONDS" -- on speaker during Beat 2 (the visitor's decision window).
- **Audio-friendly:** Screen share walkthrough described verbally: "Above the fold you see the headline, right underneath that there's a product screenshot showing an actual conversation in the app."

## BLOCK 4: "Social Proof at Zero" (10:30 - 13:30) ~710 words

**Chapter title:** How I Looked Established With Zero Customers
**Re-hook beat:** "Seventy-seven percent of landing pages have zero social proof. Which means if you add literally anything -- a screenshot, a beta testimonial, a demo video -- you already look more credible than three out of four competitors." (Works cold: specific stat, immediately actionable, surprisingly low bar.)
**Purpose:** Solve the "but I have nothing to show" problem. Show the social proof spectrum from zero to established. Prove you can always have SOMETHING. This is the payoff of the hook's promise: "you don't need revenue or testimonials to fix it."
**Estimated Length:** ~3:00 (~710 words)

### SETUP (25%, ~180 words)

- **Beat 1 (Re-hook):** "Seventy-seven percent of landing pages have zero social proof. No testimonials. No screenshots. No usage numbers. Nothing. Which means if you add literally anything, you're already ahead of most of your competition."
- **Beat 2 (The real blocker):** "But I hear this all the time. 'I have zero customers. What social proof can I possibly have?' And they're right to ask. Because most advice about social proof assumes you already have customers. 'Add testimonials.' Great. From who? 'Show your customer logos.' What customers?"
- **Beat 3 (The reframe):** "Social proof isn't about having customers. It's about proving the product is real and someone besides you has touched it. Those are two different things. And you can do both at zero revenue."

### TENSION (45%, ~320 words)

- **Beat 4 (What I actually had at zero customers):** "When DM Champ had zero paying customers, here's what I had on the landing page. Beta testimonials from people who tried the product for free. Product screenshots showing actual conversations being handled. That was it. No revenue numbers. No '500 companies trust us' badge. Just enough to prove it existed and worked."
- **Beat 5 (The social proof spectrum -- visual moment):** "Social proof scales with your stage. At zero customers: beta testimonials, product screenshots, a demo video, maybe a case study from a free trial. At five customers: real testimonials, real usage metrics, before-and-after results. At fifty customers: logos, case studies, revenue numbers, industry awards. You use whatever fits your stage. But you never use nothing."
- **Beat 6 (How to get beta testimonials at zero):** "Find three people in your target market. Let them use the product for free for a week. Ask them what they think. Use their words on your landing page. That's it. You're not faking testimonials. You're getting real feedback from real people who used a real product. The fact that they didn't pay doesn't make their experience less valid."
- **Beat 7 (Ask, don't tell #3):** "Beta testimonials. Product screenshots. A demo video. None of this requires a single paying customer. So what's actually stopping you?" Viewer confronts their own developer-identity resistance.
- **Beat 8 (The 'build in public' angle):** "There's another form of social proof most developers overlook. Showing your work. Posting about what you're building, what problems you're solving, what you're learning. Not as a marketing strategy. As proof that a real person is behind this product. People buy from people they can see."

### PAYOFF (15%, ~105 words)

- **Beat 9 (The minimum viable social proof):** "At minimum you need three things on your landing page. One: a screenshot of the product actually working. Two: at least one quote from someone who used it. Three: proof that a real human being is behind this. A face. A name. A LinkedIn profile. That's it. That's social proof at zero. And it's infinitely more than what most founders launch with, which is nothing."
- **Beat 10 (Why this matters -- natural):** After Beat 9: "The difference between 'this looks like a real product' and 'this looks like a weekend project someone abandoned' is three things on a page. Not thirty. Three."

### RETENTION (10%, ~70 words)

- **Beat 11 (Bridge to Final Block):** "Three things. LinkedIn, landing page, social proof. None of them require revenue. None of them require a team. All of them can be done today. And the gap between a founder who does all three and a founder who does none is the gap between getting the meeting and getting skipped."

### TENSION LOOP
- **Raise:** Beat 1 -- "77% have zero social proof" (viewer thinks: am I in that 77%?)
- **Tease:** Beat 2 -- "From who? What customers?" (Names the viewer's exact objection)
- **Deepen:** Beat 5 -- The spectrum. Each stage has options. You're never at truly zero.
- **Release:** Beat 9 -- "Three things on a page. Not thirty. Three."
- **Re-tension:** Beat 11 -- "The gap between getting the meeting and getting skipped." (Implies: you've fixed the presentation, but now you need distribution -- M5.)

### CREATIVE ELEMENTS
- **Slide 1:** Excalidraw "Social Proof Spectrum" -- three columns: "Zero Customers" (beta testimonials, screenshots, demo video), "5 Customers" (real testimonials, metrics, results), "50 Customers" (logos, case studies, revenue). Arrow going left to right showing progression. Title: "What to Show at Every Stage." Layout: slide_full, 16:9.
- **Slide 2:** Excalidraw "Minimum Viable Social Proof" -- three items with checkboxes: "1. Product screenshot (it works) / 2. One quote from someone who used it / 3. A real human behind it (face, name, profile)." Layout: slide_full, 16:9.
- **Text overlay:** "77%" -- on speaker during Beat 1.
- **Audio-friendly:** The social proof spectrum is listed verbally with clear staging. Works without the visual.
